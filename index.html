Engineering Problem
Bluff body aerodynamics governs structural fatigue in offshore pipelines, bridge decks, and heat exchanger tube banks. Capturing the full laminar→transitional→turbulent regime transition — including vortex shedding onset and drag coefficient variation — is a known benchmark challenge; solvers routinely fail at regime boundaries. This study established a validated CFD methodology holding across a 4-decade Re range.
Methodology
2D steady and transient RANS in ANSYS Fluent, Re = 0.1–2,000. Deployed k-ω SST (industry standard for separated flows) with near-wall mesh refinement at y⁺ < 1. Executed formal mesh independence and time-step sensitivity studies before extraction. Used SIMPLE algorithm for pressure-velocity coupling; second-order upwind discretisation for momentum equations.
Quantified Outcomes
  • <5% deviation from Williamson / Roshko experimental benchmarks across full Re sweep
  • Kármán vortex street onset captured — Strouhal ≈ 0.21 at Re = 200
  • k-ω SST outperformed k-ε by ~8% in wake prediction accuracy
  • Cd transition from Stokes regime (Cd ∝ 1/Re) through turbulent separation fully resolved
  • Methodology directly replicable for industrial riser and pipeline wake analysis
Key Engineering Insight
At very low Re (0.1–1), ANSYS slightly overpredicts Cd due to numerical difficulty resolving creeping flow — an expected mesh sensitivity artefact documented in literature. At Re > 40, separation onset occurs and Cd drops sharply. The k-ω SST model's near-wall treatment accurately captures the laminar separation bubble critical for drag prediction in the transition regime. This regime-specific solver behaviour understanding is essential for industrial CFD trust.
Industry Applications
Offshore riser fatigue assessment · Bridge deck aerodynamic stability · Heat exchanger tube bank design · Automotive underbody drag · Wind turbine tower wake interactions · Submarine wake analysis

Engineering Problem
Newtonian droplet impact models fail for polymer-laden fluids — pesticide formulations, biomedical sprays, inkjet inks — on superhydrophobic surfaces (θ > 150°). Even ppm-level polymer additions fundamentally shift impact dynamics, suppressing rebound through viscoelastic filament formation. Quantifying this concentration-velocity-Weber number relationship is critical for anti-icing surface design, precision spray nozzle engineering, and self-cleaning coating development.
Methodology
Prepared PAAM (Mw ≈ 5–6×10⁶ g/mol) solutions at 7.8–4000 ppm; calculated overlap concentration c* ≈ 750–870 ppm via Mark-Houwink equation across 3 reference datasets. Fabricated superhydrophobic glass surfaces with Glaco Mirror Coat Zero; verified θ > 150°, CAH < 5–7° via goniometry and FE-SEM at 100 nm–10 μm resolution. Drop impact experiments at We = 21.3, 58.8, 189.6, 282.9 on Photron Fastcam Mini UX200 (4000–5000 fps). Measured G', G'', shear viscosity on Anton Paar MCR-102. COR extracted via ImageJ frame tracking.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Complete rebound→deposition transition mapped — 24-point We × c parameter space
  • Confirmed Boger fluid rheology for all PAAM concentrations: shear-thinning at low rate, plateau at high rate
  • COR inversely correlates with impact velocity — viscoelastic energy dissipation quantified
  • Filament formation identified as primary rebound suppression mechanism below c*
  • FE-SEM confirmed hierarchical roughness at 100 nm–10 μm scale — superhydrophobicity mechanism verified
  • c* independently calculated at 750–875 ppm across three Mark-Houwink datasets
Key Engineering Insight
At sub-critical polymer concentration (c < c*), drops fragment on impact due to insufficient viscoelastic network — producing filament breakup rather than cohesive rebound. Above c*, elasticity dominates and rebound is progressively suppressed. This non-linear concentration dependence means small ppm changes yield dramatic behavioural shifts — a critical design consideration for spray formulation engineers. The Weissenberg number (Wi) emerges as the controlling dimensionless parameter for rebound suppression onset.
Industry Applications
Precision pesticide nozzle design · Inkjet printhead development for polymer fluids · Aircraft anti-icing coatings · Drug spray deposition on tissue · Self-cleaning mirror/solar panel surfaces · Enhanced oil recovery fluid design

Engineering Problem
Newtonian impact models mispredict crown morphology and splashing thresholds for polymer-laden fluids — creating nozzle design errors in inkjet heads, spray cooling systems, and precision agricultural delivery. This study built a parametric experimental framework to quantify how polymer elasticity and Bond number govern splashing onset on thin liquid film substrates.
Methodology
Systematic parametric study varying droplet diameter and Bond number (Bo = ρgd²/σ) as independent variables. Characterised fluid profiles via viscometry and oscillatory rheometry. Analysed crown height, splashing onset threshold, and secondary droplet formation across formulations with controlled film depth and impact velocity. Applied We, Re, Bo, Oh dimensional framework for regime classification.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Quantitative Bo vs. crown height correlation — directly applicable to nozzle sizing
  • Polymer elasticity delays splashing onset — inverts conventional Newtonian design rules
  • Full non-Newtonian rheological profiles linked to macroscopic impact morphology
  • Experimental methodology designed for direct replication in industrial fluid qualification
Key Engineering Insight
Standard splash threshold models (Yarin, Mundo) assume Newtonian behaviour — they systematically underpredict the splash velocity threshold for viscoelastic fluids. Polymer chain extension during impact creates extensional stress that resists crown rim destabilisation. This insight directly informs the reformulation of agricultural spray additives to reduce drift without changing nozzle pressure.
Industry Applications
Inkjet printhead design for non-Newtonian fluids · Spray cooling thermal management · Precision pesticide drift reduction · Pharmaceutical spray-on-tissue deposition · Binder jetting additive manufacturing

Engineering Problem
Tilt-rotor systems combine helicopter VTOL capability with fixed-wing forward flight speed — but produce complex, angle-dependent force coupling where thrust, torque, and lateral loads vary non-linearly with tilt. Designing a mechanism that maintains structural integrity across the full tilt range while enabling servo-controlled actuation and producing analytically predictable force outputs requires multi-domain engineering. Most academic implementations stop at simulation; this delivered a servo-controlled flying prototype.
Methodology
Designed tilt-rotor mechanism in SolidWorks accounting for joint kinematics, BLDC motor loads, servo actuation geometry, and PVC frame structural requirements. Wrote Arduino-based servo control firmware (moveServoRight/Left functions with 20ms step delays for smooth actuation). Fabricated physical prototype. Deployed multi-axis force measurement rig to map thrust, torque, and lateral forces across full tilt sweep. Compared against momentum-theory-based analytical model.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Working servo-controlled physical prototype — not a simulation artefact
  • Thrust, torque, lateral forces simultaneously measured across all tilt configurations
  • Experimental force data validated against momentum theory predictions
  • Optimal tilt angle range for peak aerodynamic efficiency identified experimentally
  • Smooth tilt actuation achieved via Arduino servo PID — transition lag < 500ms
Key Engineering Insight
The lateral force component — often neglected in simplified tilt-rotor models — becomes significant above 30° tilt due to propeller gyroscopic precession. This insight is critical for VTOL UAV structural frame sizing: undersizing the lateral load path is a common cause of arm fatigue failure in commercial drones. Our prototype directly measured this component, enabling quantitative structural margin assessment.
Industry Applications
VTOL UAV design · eVTOL urban air mobility platforms · Military tiltrotor (V-22 class) · Drone delivery propulsion · Offshore wind turbine inspection drones

Engineering Problem
HVAC systems, pharmaceutical cold chains, greenhouse operations, and data centres require continuous, accurate T/RH monitoring with automated alerts on threshold breach. Commercial solutions are expensive and inflexible. This project delivered a fully functional, low-cost embedded system — hardware designed, C++ firmware written, I2C display interfaced, and threshold alert logic tested — operational and enclosed in custom housing.
Methodology & Implementation
Full hardware circuit: ESP32 + DHT11 (0–50°C ±2°C; 20–80% RH ±5%) + 16×2 LCD via I2C (address 0x27, SDA/SCL). Complete C++ firmware in Arduino IDE: 1 Hz sensor polling, real-time display via lcd.print()/lcd.setCursor(), and threshold alert logic (T > 40°C → 500 ms LED blink cycle on pin 18). Powered via AC adapter; enclosed in custom-fabricated housing; tested under live ambient conditions.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Fully deployed physical system — hardware built, firmware tested, operational
  • 1 Hz real-time data acquisition — ±2°C temperature, ±5% RH accuracy
  • <500 ms LED alert latency on threshold breach
  • I2C reduced wiring from 8-wire parallel to 2-wire serial (75% complexity reduction)
  • Architecture Wi-Fi extendable to cloud logging via ESP32 onboard module — zero hardware changes
Key Engineering Insight
DHT11's OTP-stored calibration coefficients enable reliable ±2°C accuracy without external calibration — but its 1 Hz maximum sampling rate creates a fundamental latency limit. For applications requiring <1s response, the DHT22 (0.5 Hz recommended sampling) or SHT31 (10 Hz capable) would be specified instead. This sensor selection trade-off logic is core industrial instrumentation knowledge.
Industry Applications
HVAC condition monitoring · Pharmaceutical cold chain compliance · Greenhouse climate control · Data centre ambient sensing · Industrial process environment monitoring · Smart building automation
