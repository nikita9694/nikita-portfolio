01 · CAPSTONE · JAN–MAY 2025 · IIT ROPAR
Bluff Body Aerodynamics: CFD Analysis of Flow Over a Circular Cylinder
Industry-standard <5% benchmark deviation across a 4-decade Reynolds number range — laminar to turbulent — using k-ω SST turbulence modelling in ANSYS Fluent.
ANSYS Fluentk-ω SSTTurbulence ModellingVortex SheddingMesh RefinementCFDURANS
+
Engineering Problem
Bluff body aerodynamics governs structural fatigue in offshore pipelines, bridge decks, and heat exchanger tube banks. Accurately capturing the laminar-to-turbulent transition — including vortex shedding onset and drag variation — is a known benchmark challenge. Most solvers fail at regime boundaries. This study built a validated computational methodology that holds across the full transition range.
Methodology
2D steady and transient RANS in ANSYS Fluent across Re = 0.1 to 2,000. Deployed k-ω SST (industry standard for separated flows) with near-wall mesh refinement at y⁺ < 1. Ran formal mesh independence and time-step sensitivity studies before extraction. Extracted Cd, Cl, Strouhal number, and pressure coefficient distributions at each flow regime.
Quantified Outcomes
  • <5% deviation from published benchmarks (Williamson, Roshko datasets) across full Re sweep
  • Kármán vortex street onset captured — St ≈ 0.21 at Re = 200, consistent with experimental literature
  • k-ω SST with y⁺ < 1 outperformed k-ε by ~8% in wake prediction accuracy
  • Drag coefficient transition from Stokes regime through turbulent separation fully resolved
  • Simulation methodology directly replicable for industrial riser and pipeline wake analysis
Technical Depth
  • Turbulence model selection rationale for separated and transitional flows
  • Mesh independence study with formal convergence verification
  • URANS vs. steady-RANS regime switching logic
  • Drag, lift, Strouhal extraction against published experimental datasets
  • Velocity contours, streamlines, Cp distribution post-processing
Industry Applications
Offshore riser fatigue analysis · Bridge deck aerodynamic stability · Heat exchanger tube bank design · Automotive underbody drag reduction · Wind turbine tower wake interactions
02 · DEP (CP301) · JAN–MAY 2024 · SUPERVISED BY DR. DEVRANJAN SAMANTA, IIT ROPAR
Non-Newtonian Drop Impact on Mirror-Coated Superhydrophobic Surfaces
Full rebound-to-deposition transition mapped across 4 Weber numbers and 6 polymer concentrations — with 5000 fps imaging, Anton Paar rheometry, and FE-SEM surface characterisation.
Anton Paar MCR-102Photron 5000fpsFE-SEMViscoelastic FluidsSuperhydrophobicWe / Wi / El NumbersMark-Houwink
+
Engineering Problem
Standard Newtonian droplet impact models fail catastrophically for polymer-laden fluids — pesticide formulations, bio-fluids, inkjet inks, polymer coatings. On superhydrophobic surfaces (θ > 150°), Newtonian drops rebound completely; yet ppm-level polymer additions fundamentally shift impact dynamics. Quantifying this transition is critical for designing anti-icing coatings, precision spray systems, and agricultural delivery nozzles.
Methodology
Prepared aqueous PAAM (Mw ≈ 5–6×10⁶ g/mol) at 7.8–4000 ppm; calculated overlap concentration c* ≈ 750–870 ppm via Mark-Houwink equation. Fabricated superhydrophobic glass surfaces with Glaco Mirror Coat Zero; verified θ > 150°, CAH < 5–7° via contact angle goniometry and FE-SEM at 100 nm–10 μm. Ran drop impact experiments at We = 21.3, 58.8, 189.6, 282.9 on Photron Fastcam Mini UX200 at 4000–5000 fps. Measured shear viscosity, G', G'' on Anton Paar MCR-102 (CP50-2). Computed COR per concentration via ImageJ frame tracking.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Complete rebound-to-deposition map across We = 21–283 × c = 7.8–4000 ppm — 24-point parameter space
  • Confirmed Boger fluid rheology across all PAAM concentrations: shear-thinning at low rate, plateau at high rate
  • COR inversely correlates with impact velocity at all concentrations — quantified viscoelastic energy dissipation
  • Identified filament formation as the primary rebound suppression mechanism below c*
  • FE-SEM confirmed hierarchical roughness at 100 nm–10 μm scale — verified superhydrophobicity mechanism
  • c* independently calculated at 750–875 ppm across three Mark-Houwink reference datasets for cross-validation
Technical Depth
  • Oscillatory shear: G', G'' (storage/loss modulus) across frequency sweeps
  • Extensional viscosity and Protorheology characterisation
  • Mark-Houwink equation application for overlap concentration determination
  • Full dimensionless framework: We, Re, Bo, Oh, Wi, El applied systematically
  • FE-SEM sample preparation and imaging protocol (100 nm–10 μm scales)
Industry Applications
Precision pesticide nozzle design · Inkjet printhead development · Aircraft anti-icing coatings · Drug spray on tissue · Self-cleaning mirror surfaces · Enhanced oil recovery
03 · CAPSTONE · JULY–DEC 2024 · IIT ROPAR
Viscoelastic Droplet Impact Dynamics on Thin Liquid Films
Polymer elasticity suppresses crown splashing — a non-intuitive finding that inverts conventional spray design assumptions and is directly applicable to nozzle sizing.
Non-Newtonian FluidsBond NumberCrown FormationRheological CharacterisationSplashing Dynamics
+
Engineering Problem
Newtonian impact models mispredict crown morphology and splashing thresholds for polymer-laden fluids — creating design errors in inkjet heads, spray cooling systems, and agricultural nozzles. This study built a parametric experimental framework to quantify how polymer elasticity and Bond number govern splashing onset.
Methodology
Parametric study varying droplet diameter and Bond number (Bo = ρgd²/σ) as primary independent variables. Characterised fluid viscoelastic profiles via viscometry and elasticity measurements. Analysed crown height, splashing onset, and secondary droplet formation across formulations with controlled film depth and impact velocity.
Quantified Outcomes
  • Quantitative Bo vs. crown height correlation established — directly usable for nozzle sizing
  • Polymer elasticity delays splashing onset — non-intuitive result inverting standard spray assumptions
  • Full non-Newtonian rheological profiles linked to macroscopic impact morphology
  • Experimental methodology designed for direct replication in industrial fluid qualification
Technical Depth
  • Bo, We, Oh dimensional analysis for impact regime mapping
  • Non-Newtonian fluid formulation and characterisation
  • Crown morphology quantification and splashing threshold identification
  • Controlled parametric experimental design methodology
Industry Applications
Inkjet printhead design · Spray cooling thermal management · Precision pesticide drift reduction · Pharmaceutical spray-on-tissue deposition · Binder jetting in additive manufacturing
